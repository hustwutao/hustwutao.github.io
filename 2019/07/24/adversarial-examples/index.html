Adversarial Examples

As deep learning (DL) being applied in many safety-critical environments such as self-driving cars, the reliability and rebustness of DL models are highly desired. However, the recent emergence of adversarial examples posed doubts of our confidence in even state of the art DL models. In this post, let’s see some methods of creating and defensing adversarial examples.

An adversarial example is a sample of input data which has been modified very slightly in a way that the modification are imperceptible to human but can easily fool deep neural networks. The term was introduced in [1] in the context of neural networks for computer vision, now people can generate adversarial examples in other areas like NLP, speech, reinforcement learning and generative models, etc. Adversarial examples fall into the machine learning security field and have many interesting properties. This post discusses adversarial examples from 4 perspective: attack methods, defense methods, properties of adversarial examples and why adversarial examples exsit.

How to attack

Firstly, let’s see some methods to generate adversarial examples. There are a variety of methods, generally, we can regard them two types: one-step methods and iterative methods. As to how to attack the model, there are white-box attack and black-box attack.

How to defense

Now that adversarial examples raise great threat to our DL models, we have to fight with them. Unfortunately, there is not yet any known strong defense against adversarial examples. Currently, there are two directions to defend adversarial examples.

The first direction attempts to produce correct predictions on adversarial examples, while not compromising the accuracy on legitimate inputs [27, 7, 9]. The other (more recent) direction instead attempts to detect adversarial examples, without introducing too many false positives.

A major difficulty of adversarial training is that it tends to overfit to the method of adversarial example generation used at training time. For example, models trained to resist FGSM adversarial examples usually fail to resist L-BFGS adversarial examples.

Properties of adversarial examples

Adversarial examples that fool one model often fool another model, even if the two models are trained on different training examples or have different architectures.
